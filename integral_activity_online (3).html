From Area to Accumulation

An interactive activity for understanding definite and indefinite integrals using geometry and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus through a real-world water tank scenario.

Definite Integrals Indefinite Integrals Geometry FTC

1) Real-World Scenario: Filling a Water Tank

A community garden is filling a water tank for irrigation. The rate of water flow into the tank changes over time, and your job is to analyze how much water enters the tank and how the total amount of water accumulates.

Let \(f(x)\) represent the water flow rate in gallons per minute after \(x\) minutes.

This means:

  • A definite integral gives the total change in water volume over an interval of time.
  • An indefinite integral gives a family of accumulation functions for water volume.
  • The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus connects the rate function to the accumulation function.
Interpret the graph as water flow rate versus time. Then compute the total water added over the interval and describe what the geometry means.

The blue region shows accumulated change in water volume over the selected time interval.

2) Definite Integral Check

3) Indefinite Integral Check

Find an antiderivative of the selected function.

Find \(\int x\,dx\).

Use the form \(ax^n + C\) when possible for the chosen function.

4) Build the Accumulation Function

For the selected function, define

\(F(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt\)

Estimate values of \(F(x)\) at different points. Then explain how the slope of \(F\) relates to \(f\).

5) Reflection Prompts

  1. How is a definite integral different from an indefinite integral?
  2. When does the definite integral represent a positive value, a negative value, or zero?
  3. How does geometry help you evaluate an integral without using formal antiderivatives?
  4. What does the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus tell you about \(F'(x)\) if \(F(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt\)?

Teacher Notes

Success criteria:

  • Students interpret \(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) as signed area.
  • Students identify an indefinite integral as a family of antiderivatives.
  • Students connect accumulation and slope through FTC.
